Mining That Answers to the Land: DOKA's Environmental Management Framework

Before a single drill turns, DOKA's environmental obligations are already binding. Here is how our management framework translates a commitment to stewardship into daily operational discipline across all three sites.

15 Mar 2025

There is a phrase that shapes every decision DOKA makes in the field: the land is borrowed, not owned. However the law describes our license rights, we operate with the understanding that the ground we work must be returned to the communities and landscapes that surround it in at least the condition we found it. That standard is not aspirational. It is operationally enforceable, and it is enforced.

The Baseline Before the Work

Every DOKA site begins with a comprehensive environmental baseline study before any exploration equipment enters the field. Conducted by independent, accredited environmental consultancies, these studies document pre-existing ecological conditions: flora and fauna inventory, surface and groundwater quality, soil composition, ambient air, and the location of any ecologically sensitive features within the licence boundary and a defined buffer zone beyond it.

The baseline is not a formality. It is the reference point against which all subsequent monitoring is measured. If our activities affect a water quality parameter or a vegetation zone, that change is measurable against a documented pre-disturbance record and it triggers a response protocol. Without an honest baseline, environmental monitoring is theatre. With it, it is accountability.

The Environmental Management Plan

Each site operates under an approved Environmental Management Plan (EMP) submitted to and registered with the relevant Turkish regulatory authorities. The EMP defines permitted access zones, seasonal operational restrictions where wildlife activity requires them, waste handling and disposal procedures, water management requirements, and emergency response protocols for spill or incident events.

Critically, the EMP designates no-go zones: areas of elevated ecological sensitivity identified in the baseline study where no equipment, personnel, or materials may be present under any operational circumstance. These boundaries are marked physically on-site and are incorporated into contractor briefing requirements. Any contractor working on a DOKA site is legally bound by the EMP's terms as a condition of their engagement.

Water: The Non-Negotiable

Water is the environmental dimension that carries the highest risk in any mining or exploration context, and the dimension DOKA treats with the most rigorous controls. At exploration stage, the primary risk is surface water contamination from vehicle operations, fuel storage, or disturbed soil reaching drainage channels. Our protocols address each of these specifically: fuel storage is in bunded, impermeable secondary containment; vehicle servicing is carried out only at designated hard-standing points with oil-water separation; disturbed soil areas are stabilised with appropriate revegetation or physical barriers before seasonal rains.

Groundwater monitoring boreholes are established prior to operations and sampled quarterly. Results are reported to the relevant authority and retained in our environmental records, which are available to regulatory inspectors on request.

Rehabilitation From Day One

Land rehabilitation is not something that happens after a project ends. At DOKA, rehabilitation planning begins at the same time as exploration planning. For every area disturbed by track construction, drill pad preparation, or trenching, a corresponding rehabilitation prescription is defined in advance. Where ground has been disturbed during an exploration campaign, rehabilitation of that area is completed before the next campaign season begins. We do not allow a backlog of disturbed land to accumulate.

Transparency as a Standard

DOKA publishes operational updates through this Newsroom and maintains a direct communication channel with community representatives in each of the three provinces where we hold licences. Environmental monitoring summaries are available to community liaison contacts on request. We do not consider this transparency to be a gesture of goodwill: we consider it a condition of operating responsibly in someone else's landscape.
