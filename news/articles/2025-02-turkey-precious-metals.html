Turkey's Precious Metals Endowment and DOKA's Strategic Position Within It

Turkey ranks among the world's significant gold-producing nations, yet a substantial share of its geological endowment remains underexplored. Understanding why and where is the foundation of DOKA's exploration strategy.

10 Feb 2025

Turkey is not a country that most observers associate with gold. That is a significant market inefficiency, and it is precisely where DOKA's opportunity begins.

Turkey is, by measured output, one of the larger gold producers in Europe and the Middle East, ranking among the world's top twenty gold-producing nations in recent years. The country's geological endowment in silver is similarly substantial. Yet the ratio of exploration activity to the size and quality of Turkey's known mineral belts is markedly low compared to peer jurisdictions in Central Asia, West Africa, or the Americas. The implication is clear: a significant portion of Turkey's precious metals resource base has not yet been found.

The Tethyan Belt: Turkey's Geological Context

Turkey sits athwart the Tethyan metallogenic belt, a geological structure of extraordinary scale extending from the Balkans through Anatolia and into Central Asia. This belt is one of the most productive precious and base metals-bearing geological settings on Earth, hosting major gold deposits in Bulgaria, Greece, Georgia, Armenia, and Iran. In Turkey, it expresses itself across the length of the country, with known occurrences of gold, silver, copper, and other metals distributed across Thrace, the Aegean coast, Central Anatolia, the Eastern Black Sea region, and the Southeast.

The geological architecture that makes the Tethyan belt prospective is structural complexity combined with a history of hydrothermal fluid activity: the conditions under which precious metals are concentrated from dispersed crustal sources into economically workable deposits. Epithermal and porphyry systems, both represented in Turkey's known mineral inventory, are the primary deposit types defining the country's precious metals potential.

The Exploration Gap

Despite this endowment, systematic modern exploration has been applied to only a fraction of the Turkish licence area that, on geological grounds, warrants investigation. There are structural and historical reasons for this underexploration, including a regulatory environment that underwent significant reform in the 2000s and 2010s, and a relative scarcity of experienced domestic precious metals exploration companies. That scarcity is changing. The past decade has seen a meaningful increase in both domestic and international exploration activity in Turkey, and DOKA is part of this emerging exploration cohort.

DOKA's Site Selection Logic

DOKA's three licence areas in Kırşehir, Bayburt, and Yozgat were not selected opportunistically. Each was the product of a systematic geological review process evaluating hundreds of data points: historical exploration records, regional geological mapping, geochemical surveys, structural analysis, and land access conditions. Kırşehir and Yozgat sit within the Central Anatolian mineral belt, a structurally complex corridor that has yielded gold and silver occurrences at multiple locations and is underexplored relative to its geological prospectivity. Bayburt, in the Eastern Black Sea region, targets a geological setting associated with calc-alkaline magmatic systems that have produced significant gold deposits elsewhere in the region.

Strategic Significance for Turkey

Beyond the investment case, DOKA's work carries a dimension that matters to us as a Turkish company: every kilogram of gold or silver produced domestically represents a reduction in Turkey's dependence on imported precious metals and a contribution to the country's strategic minerals security. Increasing domestic production from responsibly operated mines reduces import requirements, strengthens the current account, and generates economic activity in the regions where exploration and production occur. This is not incidental to DOKA's purpose. It is constitutive of it.
